Platform Engineering vs. Traditional DevOps

Platform engineering is not DevOps with a new name. It's a shift from managing infrastructure to building internal products.

All insights
Platform Engineering 7 min read

Every few years, the industry renames something and calls it new. So when "platform engineering" started showing up everywhere, it was fair to ask: is this just DevOps with better marketing?

Having worked in both models, I don't think so. The distinction is real, and it matters for how you structure teams and what you build.

How DevOps typically works in practice

The original DevOps idea was straightforward: break down the wall between development and operations. Developers should understand how their code runs. Ops should be involved earlier in the development cycle. Shared ownership leads to better outcomes.

In practice, what happened at most organizations was different. DevOps became a job title. Companies hired "DevOps engineers" and put them on a team. That team became the new ops team, just with a different name and better tools. Developers still threw code over the wall — now the wall just had a Jenkins pipeline on it.

The DevOps team became a ticket queue. Need a new environment? File a ticket. Need a database? Ticket. Need a change to the CI pipeline? Ticket. The bottleneck shifted from "ops is slow" to "the DevOps team is overwhelmed."

What platform engineering changes

Platform engineering takes a different approach to the same underlying problem. Instead of being a service team that fulfills requests, the platform team builds products that development teams use directly.

The difference is subtle but significant:

  • A DevOps team provisions a database when asked. A platform team builds a self-service interface where developers provision their own databases, with the right defaults, security policies, and backup configurations baked in.
  • A DevOps team configures CI/CD pipelines for each project. A platform team builds a pipeline template system where teams plug in their project and get a working pipeline with standardized stages.
  • A DevOps team manages Kubernetes clusters. A platform team abstracts Kubernetes behind a developer portal where teams deploy via a config file without needing to know about pods, services, or ingress rules.

The product mindset

The biggest shift is treating the platform as an internal product. This sounds obvious, but the implications change everything about how the team operates.

If you're building a product, you need users. You talk to them. You understand their pain points. You prioritize features based on adoption, not based on which team complains loudest. You measure success by how many teams can ship independently, not by how many tickets you close.

This also means the platform team needs product skills, not just infrastructure skills. Someone needs to own the developer experience. Someone needs to decide what gets built and what doesn't. You need documentation that's actually useful, not a wiki page that was last updated eighteen months ago.

Where teams get this wrong

The most common failure mode is building a platform that nobody uses. This happens when the platform team builds what they think developers need instead of what developers actually need.

I've seen teams spend months building an elaborate self-service portal with approval workflows and RBAC policies, only to discover that developers just wanted a faster way to spin up a test environment. The fancy portal added friction instead of removing it.

The other failure mode is over-abstracting. If your platform hides so much that developers can't debug their own applications, you've traded one bottleneck for another. Good platforms provide sensible defaults but let you drop down a level when you need to.

Does your organization need this?

Not every organization needs a platform team. If you have five developers, you don't need an internal developer platform. You need good documentation and a few well-maintained scripts.

Platform engineering starts to make sense when you have multiple teams deploying independently and you notice the same problems coming up repeatedly: inconsistent deployment processes, duplicated infrastructure code, teams blocked waiting for another team to provision something.

The signal that it's time is when your DevOps team spends more than half their time on repetitive requests that could be automated, and the queue keeps growing.

Starting small

You don't need to build Backstage on day one. Start with the most painful bottleneck. If every team spends a week setting up CI/CD, build golden pipeline templates. If environment provisioning takes days, build a self-service Terraform wrapper. If developers keep misconfiguring Kubernetes manifests, build a simplified deployment interface.

Solve one problem well. Get adoption. Then solve the next one. A platform is built incrementally, not designed in a committee.
